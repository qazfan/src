Unfinite

Robot Usul

Unfinite is in his mid thirties. He is lanky with pale skin and thick black hair. He packs the same lunch for work each day and wears the same seven shirts most weeks.

Unfinite is a hard worker. He does his job, then goes home and learns about things that will be useful for work later. He also spends a great deal of time reading, and he writes short sci-fi stories for fun. He sells them as e-books, but never shows anyone he knows.

His acquaintances think of him as pleasant, but unremarkable. To them, he seems only ever to be calm or happy. He is attentive, but he does not speak often. He is reserved even with his closest friends.

Unfinite loves coding and creating things. He even loves debugging. He dislikes deadlines, though. He is enough of a perfectionist that he never feels anything is truly finished. He hates typos and problems for which he can't find a solution. He likes reading and writing, but hates books with lousy endings.

Unfinite lives on the top floor of a swanky high-rise apartment building in the heart of the city. Higher-rise might be a more appropriate description, though, since in Y1408 there aren't many short buildings left. His domicile looks almost exactly as it did the day after he moved in. He owns very little besides clothing and technology. His refrigerator holds staples, a few store-bought meals, and half a burrito. Cooking never seemed worth the time.

Unfinite is introverted. He does not go out of his way to seek company. He does keep on good terms with his colleagues, though.

His history is best summed up in a résumé.

About

Unfinite Labs began eight years ago when a crazy Usul with four hundred million Neopoints decided it would be a good idea to start a company to build a time machine like the one she believed she used to make her fortune. The people who work here tend to be somewhat nonstandard. Time travel is impossible, and we all know it, yet here we are. We research, develop, and test theories about time and about the code of the universe. We make a point of breaking those laws wherever possible. We like to do impossible things.

Our staff is a mix of physicists, researchers, and engineers. The physicists study the laws of HTML and CSS on both theoretical and experimental levels. They look for loopholes and come up with wild theories about "scripts," "databases," "queries," and "preprocessors." They say these things run Neopia on a much deeper level than the code with effects we can observe directly. When they cry out, "Eureka!" or "TAKE THAT, IE5!" you know they're on to something. Meanwhile, our researchers read hundreds of scientific papers to find out what physicists elsewhere have been discovering and what engineers elsewhere have been developing. I am one of the engineers. We turn the discoveries of the physicists and researchers into prototypes and products.


Faculty

Emenai owns Unfinite Labs, but she is only seen there a few times a year. She drops in and visits every department. Her demeanor is generally detached. She comes off as tired and sad, and it's hard to say if it is the mark of regret, a heavy burden, or a lousy sleep schedule. Unfinite likes to show her his work because, though she says very little, he gets the impression that she understands it. She skims the mundane but lingers by all the truly interesting inventions in the lab, even if they are incomplete or look like garbage.

Joseph is another engineer. He starts new projects too frequently and finishes old projects very seldom. He works quickly, though, and never seems to need manuals or references. Unfinite asks him questions frequently and sometimes picks up his discarded projects, but they don't try to be friends. They have similar jobs, but very little else in common.

Bridget is also an engineer. She leads most projects that require more than one person. She tells people what to do and they do it. She always leaves the hardest parts for herself, though, and she is always willing to work late. Unfinite likes working on her teams because she doesn't overlook things or underestimate the work that needs to be done.

Joel/Deanna/Phillip is one three-headed person in Unfinite's mind. They are the three researchers who work most closely with the engineers. They joke amongst themselves perpetually and Unfinite sometimes wonders how they accomplish any work at all. They manage it quite well one way or another, though.

You're welcome to wander around and see some of our inventions. Feel free to pick them up and play with them. You can copy the blueprints for some of them, or, if you have experience with this sort of thing, you can look at the technical overview to learn how to build your own. If you copy or closely imitate these inventions, it is your responsibility to link back to this page. We have some handy links here for your convenience.

Technical Overview

I don't remember how this is even possible. Allow me to refer to the blueprints... Ah, yes. Each digit you see is part of a table with the other nine digits, but you only see one at a time because they are in div just big enough to show one digit. That small div, which I'll call div A, is inside another div of the same size, div B. Inside div B is another table of digits. Hovering over div B makes it bigger so you can see them and select the digit you want. When you click on one, it links to the corresponding digit in the table in div A. When you click a correct digit, it links you to an entirely new set of six digits. You don't see the change most of the time because the previous correct digits are already filled in so that the new set looks exactly the same as the old set. It is important to enter the digits in order from left to right so that you are in the right set when you enter each digit. Only the right digit on the last set unlocks it, so entering it at the beginning would do nothing.

The things you have seen so far were all coded by engineers. Physicists and researchers code too for experiments, but the results are very different. They make "proof-of-concept" projects whose only purpose is to show that something is possible. Sometimes they also make "Ooh, what does this button do?" projects just to see what happens when they code things in unusual ways.
